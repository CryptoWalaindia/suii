Bitcoin in India

Quick Answer

Bitcoin in India refers to the growing awareness, education, buying, holding, and discussion of
Bitcoin among Indian users. Bitcoin is not legal tender in India like the rupee, but virtual digital
assets are taxed under Indian income tax rules.

Definition

Bitcoin in India means the use, study, ownership, taxation, and public discussion of Bitcoin
within the Indian financial and regulatory environment.

Simple Meaning

Bitcoin in India is not just about buying Bitcoin.
It includes:
● Learning what Bitcoin is
● Understanding wallets and self-custody
● Knowing Indian tax rules
● Understanding exchange risk
● Understanding RBI’s cautious view
● Comparing Bitcoin with rupee, UPI, and Digital Rupee
For Indian beginners, Bitcoin should first be treated as an education topic.

Why It Matters

India has a young, digital-first population. Millions of Indians already use UPI, mobile banking,
digital payments, and investment apps.
This makes Indians naturally curious about Bitcoin.
But Bitcoin is different from normal Indian digital payments.
UPI moves rupees between bank accounts.
Bitcoin is a global monetary network.
Digital Rupee is issued by RBI.
Bitcoin is not issued by any central bank.
Understanding these differences is important.
Legal and Tax Context in India
India taxes income from Virtual Digital Assets. The Income Tax Department explains that
income from transfer of VDAs is taxable at 30%, with no deduction other than cost of acquisition
and no set-off of losses. It also explains that 1% TDS applies under Section 194S when
payment is made to a resident for transfer of VDA, subject to thresholds.
This taxation does not mean Bitcoin is legal tender.
Legal tender in India is the Indian rupee. RBI’s Digital Rupee FAQ says e₹ is a digital form of the
rupee banknote and is legal tender issued as RBI’s liability.
What Beginners in India Should Know
A beginner in India should understand five things:
Bitcoin is volatile.
Crypto income has tax rules.
Self-custody requires responsibility.
Exchanges carry platform risk.
Bitcoin is different from UPI and Digital Rupee.
CryptoWala’s approach should be simple: education before action.

This is the right learning path.

Real-World Example

Asha is a working professional in Pune.
She uses UPI every day. She invests through mutual fund SIPs. She hears about Bitcoin from
friends.
Before buying Bitcoin, she learns:
What Bitcoin is
How wallets work
What Indian tax rules say
Why private keys matter
Why Bitcoin is not the same as UPI
This is the right learning path.

Common Misunderstanding

Many beginners think:
“If crypto is taxed, it must be fully regulated like stocks.”
That is not correct.
Taxation and full regulatory clarity are different things. India has tax rules for VDAs, but the
broader regulatory framework is still evolving.

FAQs
Is Bitcoin legal in India?
Bitcoin is not legal tender in India. However, India has tax rules for Virtual Digital Assets, including crypto assets. Is Bitcoin banned in India? As of this update, Bitcoin is not banned for holding or trading, but users must follow tax and compliance rules. Is Bitcoin the same as Digital Rupee? No. Bitcoin is decentralized and not issued by RBI. Digital Rupee is India’s CBDC issued by RBI. Do Indians have to pay tax on Bitcoin? Income from transfer of VDAs is taxable under Indian income tax rules. Users should consult a tax professional. Should beginners in India use self-custody? They can learn self-custody, but they should understand seed phrases, private keys, wallet safety, and transaction risks first.

CBDC

Quick Answer

CBDC stands for Central Bank Digital Currency. It is digital money issued by a country’s central
bank. India’s CBDC is called the Digital Rupee or e₹.

Definition

A CBDC is a digital form of central bank money issued and backed by a country’s central bank.

Simple Meaning

A CBDC is government-backed digital money.
It is not a private cryptocurrency.
It is not created by a decentralized network.
It is issued by a central bank, such as RBI in India.

Why It Matters

CBDCs matter because central banks around the world are studying how money should work in
a digital economy.
People already use digital payments every day.
But most digital payments today move bank deposits or payment balances.
A CBDC is different because it represents central bank money in digital form.
RBI’s FAQ describes Digital Rupee as India’s CBDC and says it is a digital form of the rupee
banknote.
Types of CBDC
CBDCs are usually discussed in two types:
Retail CBDC
Used by individuals and businesses for payments.
Wholesale CBDC
Used by financial institutions for settlement.
RBI’s Digital Rupee FAQ discusses both retail and wholesale CBDC.
CBDC vs Bitcoin
CBDC and Bitcoin are both digital, but they are very different.
CBDC:
Issued by central bank
Legal tender
Controlled monetary system
Usually permissioned
National currency
Bitcoin:
No central issuer
Global network
Fixed supply
Open participation
Decentralized verification

Real-World Example

Digital Rupee is India’s CBDC.
If RBI issues e₹ through participating banks, users may hold and use it through approved
wallets.
This is different from holding Bitcoin in a self-custody wallet.

Common Misunderstanding

Many beginners think CBDC means cryptocurrency.
Not exactly.
CBDC uses digital technology, but it is central bank money. Bitcoin is decentralized crypto
money. They are not the same.

FAQs
What does CBDC stand for?
CBDC stands for Central Bank Digital Currency. What is India’s CBDC called? India’s CBDC is called Digital Rupee or e₹. Is CBDC the same as Bitcoin? No. CBDC is issued by a central bank. Bitcoin is decentralized. Is CBDC legal tender? A CBDC can be legal tender depending on the country’s design. RBI says India’s e₹ is legal tender. Why are central banks exploring CBDCs? They are exploring digital money, payment efficiency, settlement, innovation, and policy use cases.

Crypto Tax in India

Quick Answer

Crypto tax in India refers to the tax rules applied to income from Virtual Digital Assets such as
cryptocurrencies. As per Income Tax Department guidance, income from transfer of VDAs is
taxed at 30%, with no deduction except cost of acquisition, and 1% TDS may apply under
Section 194S subject to thresholds.

Definition

Crypto tax in India is the tax treatment of income, gains, and transactions involving Virtual
Digital Assets under Indian income tax law.

Simple Meaning

If you make income from selling or transferring crypto in India, tax rules may apply.
The Indian tax system uses the term Virtual Digital Asset, or VDA.
This can include cryptocurrencies and certain digital tokens.
For beginners, the key point is simple:
Crypto profits are not ignored by the tax system.

Why It Matters

Crypto tax matters because many beginners focus only on price.
But taxes can affect the final outcome.
If someone buys Bitcoin and later sells it at a gain, they need to understand how that gain may
be taxed.
If TDS is deducted, they need to know where it appears and how to report it.
If they make a loss, they need to understand current loss set-off restrictions.
Current Basic Tax Treatment
According to Income Tax Department material, income from transfer of VDA is taxable at 30%.
The guidance also states that no deduction is allowed except the cost of acquisition, and no
set-off of loss is allowed.
This means beginners should not assume crypto is taxed like normal equity investments or
mutual funds.
TDS on Crypto
TDS means Tax Deducted at Source.
For VDA transfers, Section 194S can apply. Income Tax Department guidance states that 1%
TDS applies when payment is made to a resident for transfer of VDA, subject to thresholds.
This does not mean TDS is the final tax. It is tax deducted and reported. Final tax depends on
the income calculation.

Real-World Example

Rahul buys Bitcoin for ₹50,000.
Later, he sells it for ₹70,000.
His gain is ₹20,000 before considering applicable tax treatment.
Under VDA tax rules, the gain may be taxed at 30%, subject to applicable surcharge and cess.
TDS may also apply depending on the transaction.
Rahul should keep records and consult a tax professional.

Common Misunderstanding

Many beginners think 1% TDS means only 1% tax.
That is wrong.
TDS is not the same as final tax. It is a deduction at source. Final tax may still be calculated
separately.

FAQs
What is crypto tax in India?
Crypto tax in India refers to tax rules on income from transfer of Virtual Digital Assets. What is the tax rate on crypto in India? Income Tax Department guidance says income from transfer of VDA is taxable at 30%, with applicable surcharge and cess. Is 1% TDS the final crypto tax? No. TDS is not the final tax. It is tax deducted at source. Can crypto losses be set off in India? Income Tax Department guidance says no set-off of loss is allowed for income from transfer of VDA. Should I consult a CA for crypto tax? Yes. Crypto tax can be complex, especially if you have multiple trades, wallets, exchanges, or cross-border transactions.

Digital Rupee

Quick Answer

Digital Rupee, or e₹, is India’s central bank digital currency issued by the Reserve Bank of India.
It is a digital form of the rupee banknote and is legal tender.

Definition

Digital Rupee is India’s CBDC, a digital version of the Indian rupee issued as a liability of the
Reserve Bank of India.
RBI’s Digital Rupee FAQ says e₹ is a digital form of the rupee banknote, is legal tender, and is
the liability of RBI.

Simple Meaning

Digital Rupee is the rupee in digital form, issued by RBI.
It is not Bitcoin.
It is not a private cryptocurrency.
It is not a bank deposit in the same way as normal bank balance.
It is central bank money in digital form.

Why It Matters

Digital Rupee matters because it shows India is experimenting with the future of money and
payments.
India already has strong digital payments through UPI.
Digital Rupee is different because it is issued directly by the central bank as digital legal tender.
The RBI retail digital rupee pilot began on December 1, 2022, within a closed user group of
participating customers and merchants.
Digital Rupee vs UPI
UPI is a payment system that moves money between bank accounts.
Digital Rupee is money itself in digital form.
Simple comparison:
UPI = payment rail
Digital Rupee = digital central bank money
This distinction is important.
Digital Rupee vs Bitcoin
Digital Rupee is issued by RBI.
Bitcoin is not issued by any central bank.
Digital Rupee is legal tender in India.
Bitcoin is a decentralized digital asset, not Indian legal tender.
Digital Rupee supply and design are controlled by RBI.
Bitcoin supply follows protocol rules.

Real-World Example

If Asha pays a shopkeeper through UPI, money moves between bank accounts.
If she pays using Digital Rupee, she uses RBI-issued digital currency through a supported
wallet or system.
Both may feel digital, but the underlying structure is different.

Common Misunderstanding

Many beginners think Digital Rupee is India’s Bitcoin.
It is not.
Digital Rupee is a central bank digital currency. Bitcoin is decentralized and global.

FAQs
What is Digital Rupee?
Digital Rupee, or e₹, is India’s CBDC issued by RBI. Is Digital Rupee legal tender? Yes. RBI says e₹ is legal tender and a liability of RBI. Is Digital Rupee the same as UPI? No. UPI is a payment system. Digital Rupee is digital central bank money. Is Digital Rupee the same as Bitcoin? No. Digital Rupee is issued by RBI. Bitcoin is decentralized and not issued by a central bank. Can everyone use Digital Rupee? Access depends on RBI pilots, participating banks, wallets, and rollout status.

RBI and Crypto

Quick Answer

RBI has generally taken a cautious view of private cryptocurrencies because of concerns
around financial stability, consumer protection, money laundering, and monetary control. At the
same time, RBI supports India’s own central bank digital currency, the Digital Rupee.

Definition

RBI and crypto refers to the Reserve Bank of India’s position, warnings, policy concerns, and
regulatory approach toward private cryptocurrencies and digital assets.

Simple Meaning

RBI is India’s central bank.
It manages important parts of the financial system, including:
● Monetary policy
● Currency issuance
● Banking regulation
● Financial stability
● Payment systems
Because crypto assets can affect money, payments, and financial behaviour, RBI pays close
attention to them.
RBI’s Main Concerns
RBI has repeatedly warned users about risks in virtual currencies.
RBI’s earlier caution note on virtual currencies highlighted risks related to digital wallets,
hacking, loss of password, malware, absence of authorized central agency, volatility, and
legal/customer protection concerns.
RBI’s concerns usually include:
● Consumer protection
● Fraud and scams
● Market volatility
● Money laundering risk
● Financial stability
● Currency and monetary policy risk
● Lack of underlying issuer in private crypto assets
RBI and Digital Rupee
RBI is cautious about private cryptocurrencies, but it supports Digital Rupee.
Digital Rupee is different because it is issued by RBI itself.
RBI’s FAQ says e₹ is India’s CBDC, a digital form of the rupee banknote, legal tender, and RBI’s
liability.
Reuters reported in May 2026 that RBI planned to expand Digital Rupee use cases, including
welfare schemes and cross-border payment pilots, based on its 2025–26 annual report.
RBI View vs Indian Tax Rules
Beginners should understand this clearly:
RBI’s caution on crypto is one thing.
Income tax rules on VDAs are another thing.
Taxation does not mean RBI treats Bitcoin like rupee.
It only means income from VDA transfers is taxable under Indian law.

Real-World Example

Asha hears that crypto is taxed in India.
She assumes RBI approves crypto like it approves rupee payments.
That is not correct.
India can tax VDA income while RBI still warns about risks in private crypto assets.

Common Misunderstanding

Many people think RBI and the Government of India have exactly the same role.
They do not.
RBI is the central bank.
The government makes laws and tax policy.
Regulators and agencies handle different parts of compliance.
Crypto regulation can involve multiple institutions.

FAQs
What is RBI’s view on crypto?
RBI has generally taken a cautious view and has warned users about risks in private virtual currencies. Does RBI issue Bitcoin? No. Bitcoin is not issued by RBI or any central bank. Does RBI issue Digital Rupee? Yes. Digital Rupee is India’s CBDC issued by RBI. Is Digital Rupee the same as crypto? No. Digital Rupee is central bank money. Private crypto assets like Bitcoin are different. Does crypto tax mean RBI approves crypto? No. Taxation and central bank approval are different issues.

TDS on Crypto

Quick Answer

TDS on crypto in India generally refers to 1% tax deducted at source under Section 194S on
payments made to a resident for the transfer of a Virtual Digital Asset, subject to specified
thresholds.

Definition

TDS on crypto is the tax deducted at source when certain payments are made for the transfer of
Virtual Digital Assets under Indian income tax rules.

Simple Meaning

TDS means tax is deducted before the seller receives the full payment.
In crypto, this was introduced to create transaction reporting and tax compliance.
It does not mean the government is charging only 1% total tax.
It means 1% may be deducted at source and reported.

Why It Matters

TDS matters because it affects cash flow and tax reporting.
If you trade frequently, TDS can add up across transactions.
It also creates a record that may appear in your tax documents.
This is why beginners should track all transactions carefully.
Section 194S in Simple Words
Section 194S deals with TDS on payment for transfer of Virtual Digital Assets.
Income Tax Department guidance states:
● 1% TDS applies when payment is made to a resident for transfer of VDA
● No TDS is required if payment does not exceed ₹50,000 for specified persons
● No TDS is required if payment does not exceed ₹10,000 for other payers
These thresholds and rules should be checked before filing or transacting because tax rules can
change.

Real-World Example

Asha sells crypto worth ₹20,000 on a platform.
Depending on the platform, transaction structure, and applicable rules, TDS may be deducted
and reported.
This TDS is not necessarily her final tax. She still needs to calculate total income and report
correctly.

Common Misunderstanding

Many beginners think:
“1% TDS means my crypto tax is done.”
That is not correct.
TDS is only a deduction at source. Final tax may be different depending on gains, income, and
reporting.
Beginner Record-Keeping Tips
Keep records of:
Buy date
Sell date
Buy price
Sell price
Exchange used
TDS deducted
Wallet transfers
Transaction IDs
Good records make tax filing easier.

FAQs
What is TDS on crypto?
TDS on crypto is tax deducted at source on certain VDA transfers. What is the TDS rate on crypto in India? Income Tax Department guidance states 1% TDS under Section 194S for payments to residents for transfer of VDA, subject to thresholds. Is TDS the same as income tax? No. TDS is deducted at source. Final tax calculation is separate. Does TDS apply to every crypto transaction? It depends on transaction type, amount, parties involved, and applicable thresholds. Where can I see deducted TDS? It may appear in tax records such as Form 26AS or AIS, depending on reporting.

UPI vs Bitcoin

Quick Answer

UPI is a payment system used to move Indian rupees between bank accounts. Bitcoin is a
decentralized monetary network and digital asset that works without a central bank. UPI moves
fiat money; Bitcoin is its own digital asset.

Definition

UPI vs Bitcoin is a comparison between India’s instant payment rail and Bitcoin’s decentralized
monetary network.

Simple Meaning

UPI and Bitcoin can both feel digital.
But they are very different.
When you use UPI, you are sending rupees.
When you use Bitcoin, you are sending Bitcoin.
UPI depends on banks, payment apps, NPCI infrastructure, and the Indian financial system.
Bitcoin depends on wallets, nodes, miners, proof of work, and the Bitcoin network.

Why It Matters

Many Indian beginners understand UPI very well.
So they naturally ask:
“Why do we need Bitcoin when UPI is already fast?”
This is a good question.
The answer is that UPI and Bitcoin solve different problems.
UPI is excellent for fast rupee payments inside India.
Bitcoin is a global digital monetary network with fixed supply and no central issuer.

Real-World Example

Asha pays ₹500 to a shopkeeper using UPI.
Her bank balance reduces. The shopkeeper’s bank balance increases.
Now imagine Asha sends Bitcoin to Rahul.
No bank balance moves. A Bitcoin transaction is created and broadcast to the Bitcoin network.
Both are digital transactions, but they are built on different systems.

Common Misunderstanding

Many beginners think Bitcoin is trying to replace UPI.
That is not the best way to understand it.
UPI is a payment rail. Bitcoin is a monetary network. They can be compared, but they are not
the same category.
Where Digital Rupee Fits
Digital Rupee is also different from both.
UPI moves bank money.
Digital Rupee is RBI-issued digital legal tender.
Bitcoin is decentralized digital money.
For Indian learners, this three-way difference is very important.

FAQs
Is UPI better than Bitcoin?
They are built for different purposes. UPI is faster and easier for rupee payments in India. Bitcoin is a decentralized global monetary network. Does UPI use blockchain? No. UPI does not require blockchain to work. Can Bitcoin be used for payments? Yes, but Bitcoin payments work differently from UPI and may involve fees, confirmations, or Lightning Network. Is Bitcoin legal tender like rupee? No. Bitcoin is not legal tender in India. Why learn Bitcoin if UPI already works? Because Bitcoin is not only a payment app. It is also a global monetary network with different rules and properties.

Virtual Digital Asset

Quick Answer

A Virtual Digital Asset, or VDA, is a term used in Indian tax law for certain digital assets,
including cryptocurrencies and some tokens. The term is important because Indian crypto tax
rules are written around VDAs.

Definition

A Virtual Digital Asset is a category under Indian tax law that includes certain cryptographic
digital assets, tokens, and related digital representations of value.

Simple Meaning

VDA is the Indian tax system’s word for many crypto-type digital assets.
Instead of saying only “crypto,” the law uses a wider term: Virtual Digital Asset.
This can include more than just Bitcoin.
It may include cryptocurrencies, tokens, and certain NFTs depending on the law and
notifications.

Why It Matters

VDA matters because India’s crypto tax rules use this term.
When people talk about crypto tax in India, they are usually talking about tax on VDAs.
If a digital asset falls under the VDA definition, special tax rules may apply.
The Income Tax Department’s VDA guidance explains taxation on transfer of VDAs and TDS
under Section 194S.
VDA vs Bitcoin
Bitcoin can be treated as a VDA for Indian tax purposes.
But Bitcoin and VDA are not the same type of word.
Bitcoin = a specific decentralized digital asset
VDA = a legal/tax category used in India
This difference is important.

Real-World Example

Asha sells Bitcoin on an exchange.
For tax reporting, that Bitcoin transaction may be treated as a transfer of a Virtual Digital Asset.
So even though she thinks of it as Bitcoin, the tax form may use the VDA category.

Common Misunderstanding

Many beginners think VDA means the government created a new type of coin.
That is not correct.
VDA is a legal/tax term. It describes a category of digital assets for taxation and compliance.

FAQs
What does VDA stand for?
VDA stands for Virtual Digital Asset. Is Bitcoin a VDA in India? For Indian tax purposes, Bitcoin can fall under the VDA framework. Is VDA the same as cryptocurrency? Not exactly. Cryptocurrency can be a type of VDA, but VDA is a broader legal/tax category. Why does India use the term VDA? The term allows tax law to cover a wider range of digital assets, not only one coin or token. Are VDAs legal tender in India? No. Being taxed as a VDA does not make an asset legal tender.
